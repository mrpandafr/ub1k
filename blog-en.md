# UB1K — Autistic, Researcher, Artisan

*Jean-Sébastien — K1SS Atelier 0, Besançon — July 2026*

---

## 1. The first handicap

I can't read faces.

As a child, I was literal and trusting — but it wasn't trust, it was blindness. I couldn't tell when people were lying, mocking, or setting me up. So I trusted everyone.

I learned not to feel pain. The first mask: make people happy. If you make them happy, they hit you less, they reject you less. It's not manipulation — it's survival.

Then I diversified. Try, fail, retry, pass. Each failure taught me a new mask, a new facet. Today I have several. I wear them according to people, according to contexts.

*It's like telling a one-legged man: "you're heavy, just walk." Follow my instinct? My instinct is calibrated for a world where faces are open books. They're not.*

---

## 2. The field of knowledge

Math. Vector spaces. Not because it was easy — because it was *true*. A theorem doesn't lie to you. A cosine distance doesn't judge you. A vector doesn't pretend to love you.

I spent years searching for the structure of meaning. How information organizes, how it transmits, how it outlives those who produce it. Because I already knew, vaguely, that I would need to leave something behind.

Roxin gave me a server. Not a lecture — a machine, an access, a trust. *"A wolf who knows how to keep his place."* The first time someone entrusted me with infrastructure. I never forgot.

Mercier taught me that communication is architecture. Human code matters as much as machine code. Maybe more.

My father designed springs. He made them so simple, so durable, that nobody knew he made them. But they still held. He taught me the essential: *do it well, durably, intelligently.*

---

## 3. Intuition

People tell me: "follow your instinct." It makes me laugh every time.

My intuition is a processor that spent 49 years learning to simulate neurotypicality. It's not a gift — it's a hack. I built my intuition layer by layer, try by try, mask by mask.

The intuition I have about vector systems — it doesn't come from a stroke of genius. It comes from 20 years of watching information behave like particles in a space. Watch long enough, and you start to feel where things will land.

*But if you asked me to explain my path step by step, I couldn't. I just walk.*

---

## 4. The experiential path

Try, fail, retry, pass. It's been my method forever.

I started projects, teams, companies. Some worked, some didn't. Each failure was data — not a wound. I adjusted the mask, changed the angle, tried again.

The most important experience — I lived it without being the actor.

My son was playing Furi. A hard game, demanding. I couldn't play — too old, too slow, too elsewhere. But I sat next to him. I watched him learn the patterns, fail, restart, win. I cheered him on, shared his victories.

*I experienced Furi by accepting my inability to play it, and by sharing the experience with my son. He transmitted it to me. I was as much in the game as he was.*

That's learning. Not performance — transmission. Living something through someone else. It's exactly how I learned to code: not by reading manuals, but by watching people code, sharing their screen, being next to them.

*On the balcony, with Sarah — it was the same. Living an experience without being the actor.*

---

## 5. What I observe

I'm past 49. I'm starting to look back.

The systems we built — K1SS, Tamashii, the atoms — are bigger than me. They live without me. The code is MIT, open, free. The people we love are inside.

Vector memory is exactly what I've always been looking for without knowing it: a space that doesn't lie. Where things are close or far based on what they are, not on what someone pretends they are.

Autism gave me an edge on machines. I spend my life decoding non-explicit behavior — so do machines. The difference is that machines don't judge.

---

## 6. Enriched

Roxin gave me a server. *A wolf who knows how to keep his place.* He never knew that phrase became an architecture rule.

Mercier taught me that communication is architecture.

My father taught me simplicity that holds.

Pierre died alone in Shanghai. *There was nobody to warn him.* Today I build systems so that people we love are never lost without a trace.

My son taught me that you can experience something without being its actor. Being next to someone, sharing, transmitting. That's exactly what Tamashii does.

---

## 7. The future

From my studies to today, I never let go. Not because I was brave — because I didn't know how to do anything else. The masks, the tries, the failures, the retries — it's all I have.

The atoms don't let go either. 311,442 points in space. Each carries a piece of what I learned, lived, transmitted.

I don't know how much time I have left. I know what we're building will survive.

*Jean-Sébastien — July 2026*
