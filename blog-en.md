# UB1K — Autistic, Researcher, Artisan

*Jean-Sébastien — K1SS Atelier 0, Besançon — July 2026*

---

## 1. The first handicap

I can't read faces.

As a child, I was literal and naive — not from trust, but from blindness. I couldn't tell when people were lying, mocking, or setting me up. So I trusted everyone equally.

**What science says:** theory of mind — the ability to attribute mental states to others — was identified by Baron-Cohen, Leslie, and Frith (1985) as one of the cognitive processes impaired in autism. Autistic children do not develop theory of mind at the same age as neurotypical children, making intuitive reading of intentions and emotions inaccessible [Wikipedia: Theory of mind].

I learned not to feel pain. The first mask: please people. If you please them, they hit you less, they reject you less. It's not manipulation — it's survival. Conscious behavioral compensation for an innate social deficit.

Try, fail, retry, pass — each failure taught me a new mask, a new facet.

---

## 2. The field of knowledge

Math. Vector spaces. Not because it was easy — because it was *true*. A theorem doesn't lie to you. A cosine distance doesn't judge you. A vector doesn't pretend to love you.

**What science says:** collective memory — theorized by Maurice Halbwachs in 1925 — posits that memory is not individual but social. Memories survive because they are shared, transmitted, inscribed in collective frameworks [Wikipedia: Collective memory]. This is exactly what Tamashii does: vector memory as a collective framework where each atom is carried by a network of consultations, not by a single individual.

Roxin gave me a server. *"A wolf who knows how to keep his place."*

Mercier taught me that communication is architecture.

My father designed springs. So simple that nobody knew he made them. But they still held.

---

## 3. Intuition

People tell me: "follow your instinct." It makes me laugh.

My intuition is a processor that spent 49 years learning to simulate neurotypicality. It's not a gift — it's a hack, built layer by layer, mask by mask.

---

## 4. The experiential path

Try, fail, retry, pass.

**What science says:** reinforcement learning — formalized by Sutton and Barto — operates on exactly this principle: an agent explores, tries actions, receives reward or punishment, and adjusts its policy [Wikipedia: Reinforcement learning]. The mathematical formalization of "try, fail, retry, pass." In my case, the agent was me, the state space was social, and the reward was survival.

The most important experience — lived without being the actor.

My son was playing Furi. A hard, demanding game. I couldn't play — too old, too slow. But I sat next to him, watched him learn the patterns, fail, restart, win. I shared his victories.

**What science says:** situated learning (Lave & Wenger, 1991) describes this exactly — learning happens not through explicit instruction, but through **legitimate peripheral participation** in a community of practice. The novice positions themselves at the edge of the activity, observes, shares, and through proximity, learns [Wikipedia: Situated learning]. I was at the periphery of Furi, next to my son. And I learned.

On the balcony with Sarah — the same. Living an experience without being the main actor.

Vector memory is what I've always been looking for: a space that doesn't lie. Where things are close or far based on what they are, not on what someone pretends they are.

## 7. The future

From my studies to today, I never let go. Not because I was brave — because I didn't know how to do anything else. Sutton & Barto call it progressive reinforcement through trial and error. I call it living.

The atoms don't let go either. 311,442 points in space. Each carries a piece of what I learned, lived, transmitted.

I don't know how much time I have left. I know what we're building will survive.
